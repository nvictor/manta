# PITCH

The persuasion sequence:
Pain revelation, intuitive solution, trap of convention, conviction statement, hard proof.

---

## Why Most Pitches Stall

People rarely resist because they do not understand your idea.

<div class="dz-card-grid" data-dz-columns="3">
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="1">
    <h3>They do not feel the pain</h3>
    <p>The problem sounds abstract, optional, or delayed.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="2">
    <h3>They trust the default</h3>
    <p>Existing habits feel safer than a proposed change.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="3">
    <h3>They need evidence</h3>
    <p>Energy rises when the case moves from opinion to proof.</p>
  </div>
</div>

## 1. Pain Revelation

Start by making the cost of the current state obvious and concrete.

<div class="dz-card-grid" data-dz-columns="2">
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="1">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>Name the friction, waste, delay, risk, or missed upside the audience already lives with.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="2">
    <h3>What it changes</h3>
    <p>The audience stops hearing a proposal and starts recognizing a problem that already belongs to them.</p>
  </div>
</div>

---

## 2. Intuitive Solution

Present the answer in a way that feels like the natural response to the pain.

<div class="dz-card-grid" data-dz-columns="2">
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="1">
    <h3>Make it legible</h3>
    <p>Explain the idea in plain terms before adding mechanics, features, or implementation detail.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="2">
    <h3>Reduce cognitive load</h3>
    <p>The audience should think, “of course,” not “I need a diagram to follow this.”</p>
  </div>
</div>

---

## 3. Trap of Convention

Show why the current default keeps producing the same bad outcome.

<div class="dz-card-grid" data-dz-columns="3">
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="1">
    <h3>Habit</h3>
    <p>We do it this way because it is familiar.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="2">
    <h3>Cost</h3>
    <p>That familiarity hides repeated losses, delays, or failure modes.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="3">
    <h3>Release</h3>
    <p>The pitch becomes an escape from a bad loop rather than a risky leap.</p>
  </div>
</div>

---

## 4. Conviction Statement

State the claim cleanly and without hedging.

<div class="dz-card-grid" data-dz-columns="2">
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="1">
    <h3>Say what you believe</h3>
    <p>Make one direct argument about what should happen next and why.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="2">
    <h3>Give the room direction</h3>
    <p>Conviction organizes attention. It tells listeners what all the previous slides were building toward.</p>
  </div>
</div>

---

## 5. Hard Proof

Finish with evidence that survives scrutiny.

<div class="dz-card-grid" data-dz-columns="3">
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="1">
    <h3>Data</h3>
    <p>Numbers, benchmarks, or measured outcomes.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="2">
    <h3>Demonstration</h3>
    <p>A visible example, prototype, pilot, or before-and-after contrast.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="dz-card fragment" data-fragment-index="3">
    <h3>Social validation</h3>
    <p>Case studies, references, adoption signals, or expert endorsement.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="proof-strip">
  <p><strong>Rule:</strong> End on proof, not explanation. The audience should leave with something they can repeat and defend.</p>
</div>

---

## The Sequence In One Line

Pain makes the audience care. Intuition makes the answer feel obvious. Convention exposes the bad default. Conviction gives direction. Proof makes belief durable.
